About me


I am an analyst at Statistics Canada with experience working on large data science projects for clients such as Health Canada and the Public Health Agency of Canada (PHAC). Natural language processing is my research focus, however I enjoy reading and conducting projects on all facets of data science. I am enthusiastic and passionate about data science and am credited with starting the first Kaggle Competitive Group at Statistics Canada.

I completed my B.Econ with a minor in Statistics at Carleton University. My undergraduate thesis The Impact of Substance Abuse in Tennessee: The Relationship between Low Birth Weight and Substance Use During Pregnancy was advised by Michel Demers. It examines the percentage of low birth weights as a result of neonatal abstinence syndrome (NAS) among each county and between the years 2013 to 2016. The aim was to further understand how the opioid crisis has affected society. I am currently exploring Graduate studies for Computer Science.

I currently work with the Longitudinal and International Study of Adults (LISA) survey as an analyst on the survey processing and dissemination team. I first joined LISA in the summer of 2018 as a student and have been with the team for all years besides the 2020 year, where I joined the Data Science Division for a one year "rotation". At LISA, I spend my time split between solving complex issues that may arise in the survey process, including producing solutions to identifying duplicate respondents, and conducting studies on various socioeconomic themes, such as food insecurity. I often pitch how data science and automation can be used to speed up the survey processes and have successfully implemented data science tools into the LISA pipeline. These include anomaly detection, North American Industry Classification System (NAICS) code classification, and IBM-WATSON ICD-10 code classification.
